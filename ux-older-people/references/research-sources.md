# Research Sources — UX Older People Skill

> Full bibliography supporting every rule in SKILL.md.
> Organized by topic area for easy cross-referencing.

## Touch Targets & Motor

- **Jin, Z. X., Plocher, T., & Kiff, L. (2007)**. Touch Screen User Interfaces for Older Adults: Button Size and Spacing. *Proceedings of the 4th International Conference on Universal Access in HCI*, Beijing, China. **Finding**: 15mm square/circle buttons + 3mm spacing = optimal efficiency and lowest error rate for older adult users.

- **Yeh, P. (2025)**. Exploring Touchscreen Interface Design for Home Health Management Products: Effects of Button Size and Position on Older Adult Users. *Human Factors and Ergonomics Society*. **Finding**: 220 subjects (110 older adults 65+). Best performance at 16mm button size; upper or right display position. Age significantly affected both operation time and error rates.

- **Hwangbo, H., Yoon, S.H., Jin, B.S., Han, Y.S., & Ji, Y.G. (2013)**. A Study of Pointing Performance of Older Adult Users on Smartphones. *Int. J. Human-Computer Interaction*, 29(9), pp. 604-618. Yonsei University, Seoul, Korea. **Finding**: Smaller targets and closely spaced icons increase error rates and slow interaction. Steep drop in errors from 5mm to 8mm, smaller improvement from 8mm to 12mm.

- **Shao et al. (2023)**. Offset models for older adult touch accuracy. **Finding**: Right-handed older adult users systematically deviate rightward when tapping; tremors intensify this. Proposed automatic correction models.

- **Systematic Approach to Characterizing Smartphone Icon-Touching Performance (2025)**. *MDPI*. **Finding**: 20% accuracy increase per 1mm of target size between 2-6mm. Above 6mm, hit rate stays >80% but increases level off (~4% per mm).

- **Harte, R. et al. (2017)**. Recommended button areas no smaller than 200mm² (~89×89dp for square target) specifically for older users.

## Typography & Readability

- **Morey et al.** Recommended 30pt for critical text, 20pt for secondary text in apps for older adults.

- **WCAG 2.1/2.2**. Contrast ratio: 4.5:1 minimum (AA), 7:1 enhanced (AAA). Line height 1.5× font size. Paragraph spacing 2× font size. Letter spacing 0.12× font size.

- **Toptal Guide (2023)**. Many older adults use reading glasses or opt for much larger font sizes. Blues can appear faded, reducing contrast. Minimum 16px body text, but go higher for readability.

- **Adchitects Guide (2025)**. Start at 16px body text, don't hesitate to go higher. Use sans-serif (Arial, Verdana, Helvetica). Avoid decorative flourishes.

- **Salesforce UX / Medium (2022)**. Body copy minimum 16px. Medium.com itself uses 20px body text. Line height 150% or 1.5× recommended by WCAG.

## Color & Contrast

- **Sethi, T. & Ziat, M. (2023)**. Dark mode vogue: Do light-on-dark displays have measurable benefits? *Ergonomics*, 66(12), 1814-1828. **Finding**: Older adults favor positive polarity (light mode) to minimize mental fatigue; younger adults prefer negative polarity in dim environments for aesthetic reasons.

- **Tandfonline (2025)**. The dark side of the interface. 173 participants. **Finding**: Cognitive scores were higher in light mode vs. dark mode. Younger adults performed significantly better than older adults in light mode.

- **While & Sarvghad (2024)**. Dark mode or light mode? Exploring contrast polarity impact on visualization performance between age groups. **Finding**: Polarity preference varied on individual basis; both polarities benefited comparable proportions.

- **Liu & Qu (2025)**. Research on Interface Visual Design Features of Mobile News on Cognitive Load: Older Adult Users in China. *Behavioral Sciences* / PMC. **Finding**: Visual complexity significantly impacts cognitive load. Black font on light backgrounds resulted in lower contrast than expected. Red keywords used as accent color were more effective for older adults.

- **Age-Centric Chromatics (2024)**. University of Edinburgh / Zhejiang University. Analysis of 10 apps for older adults. **Finding**: Mismatch between current color design decisions and actual visual needs of older adult users.

## Icons & Visual Language

- **Chen, H. et al. (2020) / ScienceDirect**. Skeuomorphic or flat icons for efficient visual search by younger and older adults? 24 older + 24 younger participants. **Finding**: Older adults searched skeuomorphic icons more quickly and accurately. They appreciated skeuomorphic icons more aesthetically. Chinese text labels improved search performance.

- **Cho & Kwon / Semantic Scholar**. The Elders Preference for Skeuomorphism as App Icon Style. **Finding**: Flat design is slower or less accurate for older adults across visual search, identifying clickable objects, and multi-page navigation.

- **PMC (2022)**. How Do Older Adults Process Icons in Visual Search Tasks? 54 medical icons tested. **Finding**: All older adults performed poorly with flat icons alone. SI + text (skeuomorphic + text) showed smallest performance difference between cognitively normal and MCI groups.

- **Developing Icons for Older Adults (2024)**. ScienceDirect. **Finding**: Older adults identify icons that are more skeuomorphic, familiar, simpler, and semantically closer to their meaning.

- **NNGroup (2026)**. The Hamburger-Menu Icon Today: Is it Recognizable? **Finding**: Label the icon "Menu." Scale appropriately. Make it look clickable. Extreme flat design obscures discoverability.

## Navigation & Cognitive Load

- **Gomez-Hernandez, M., Ferre, X., Moral, C., & Villalba-Mora, E. (2023)**. Design Guidelines of Mobile Apps for Older Adults: Systematic Review and Thematic Analysis. *JMIR mHealth and uHealth*. UPM (Spain) / Monash (Australia) / Tongji (China). The most comprehensive guideline set. 
  - Guideline 8: "Reduce the number of available elements and options in the screen."
  - Favor control tapping over gestures.
  - Provide contextual help at point of need.
  - Increase timeouts for all interactions.
  - Provide multisensory feedback.
  - Reduce touch sensitivity to prevent involuntary taps.
  - Learning by exploration is not suitable — provide proactive help.

- **Jiang, Q. et al. (2024)**. User-Centered Design Strategies for Age-Friendly Mobile News Apps. China. **Finding**: Three priority factors (by entropy weight): 1) Usability and navigation (highest priority), 2) Visual presentation and readability, 3) Interaction and touch controls. Font sizes 12-14pt; static menus; minimal gestures.

- **Toptal (2023)**. Progressive disclosure and minimalist design prevent cognitive overload. Don't divide attention across multiple tasks/parts of screen. Keep "return" and "home" readily accessible as safe points.

- **eSEOspace (2025)**. One Primary Action Per Screen: design each screen for a single focused task. Lower tolerance for errors: older users blame themselves and abandon rather than troubleshoot.

## Input & Forms

- **Soares Guedes et al.** via JMIR (2023). Almost all participants aged 60-76 had difficulty using virtual keyboards — keys too small, require precision.

- **Awan (2021)**. Usability Barriers for Older Adult Users in Smartphone App Usage. Wiley. **Finding**: Touch screen and QWERTY keyboard identified as one of 6 critical barriers.

- **JMIR (2020)**. Design and Usability Evaluation of Mobile Voice-Added Food Reporting for Older Adults: RCT. **Finding**: Voice-only reporting was significantly faster (P<.001) than button-based. Button group had 10.3% error rate from failed taps.

- **NNGroup (2024)**. How Screen-Reader Users Type on and Control Mobile Devices. **Finding**: Users tolerate dictation challenges to avoid the difficult process of typing on a touchscreen keyboard.

- **Rau et al. (2014)**. Older Adults' Text Entry on Smartphones and Tablets. 32 older adults, 4 screen sizes. **Finding**: On small displays (3.5"/5"), handwriting was faster and perceived easier than typing. Preference was 1.5× stronger for 7" vs. 5".

- **ScienceDirect (2020)**. Effects of text input methods: numeric keypad design for older adults. 51 participants ≥65. Tested 45 keypad designs (3 shapes × 5 sizes × 3 spacings). **Finding**: Optimal numeric keypad: 12.5-17.5mm button size.

## Onboarding & Help

- **UserGuiding**. Senior-Friendly Technology: Onboarding Older Adults. **Finding**: Slow, thorough guides; explain step by step what to do and why. Provide templates to minimize extra steps. Safety and risk mitigation come up time and again in research.

- **NNGroup (2024)**. Define Techy Words for Old Users. **Finding**: Many seniors unsure about terms like "URL", "homepage", "website". Only 25% received formal web training; rest were self-taught.

- **Wang et al.** Perceived usefulness is the most important predictor of technology acceptance among older adults, including those with cognitive impairment. Related to satisfaction with social and emotional needs.

## Error Prevention & Forgiving Design

- **NNGroup (2024)**. Usability for Senior Citizens. **Finding**: Interfaces are often inflexible and unforgiving of errors. Many sites accept inputs in only one form.

- **LogRocket (2025)**. UX for Reversible Actions: Decision Framework. **Finding**: Reversibility is one of the strongest trust signals in UX. Safety changes how users interact.

- **Worky (2024)**. UX Design Principles for Seniors Part II. **Finding**: Many older adults have sincere concern they will "break" technology or make irreversible errors. Easy undo/redo + feedback messaging reassures them.

- **Cadabra Studio (2025)**. Confirmations for critical actions and "undo" options help users feel safe exploring without fear of irreversible mistakes.

## Haptic Feedback

- **PMC (2025)**. Haptic-Driven Serious Card Games for Older Adults: User Preferences Study. 250 participants, average age 64.78. **Finding**: 63.2% preferred slight haptic feedback; 78.4% believed haptic + card games improve cognitive abilities; 73.6% believed it reduced visual information reliance.

## Privacy, Security & Trust

- **IEEE Spectrum (2025)**. Are Health Apps Failing Older Adults on Privacy? **Finding**: 79% of health apps lacked breach notification protocols. Average privacy policy readability: 12th grade; average older adult reading level: 8th grade. Zero apps had accessibility accommodations in privacy interfaces.

- **PMC (2024)**. Design Considerations for Mobile Health Applications Targeting Older Adults. **Finding**: Cybercriminals steal ~$40B from US older adults annually. 67% have been scam/hack victims. Privacy concerns sometimes outweigh perceived benefits.

- **ArXiv (2024)**. Evaluating Privacy Measures in Healthcare Apps Predominantly Used by Older Adults. 22 surveyed + 9 interviewed (65+). **Finding**: 63.64% express serious concerns about unauthorized data access; only 13.64% feel confident in existing protections.

- **Scientific Reports / Nature (2025)**. Analysis of user behavior under age-friendly adaptation mode (China). **Finding**: Companies must enhance security reminders, protect privacy notifications, avoid inducing unwanted downloads/purchases.

## Digital Anxiety & Trust

- **JMIR Aging (2026)**. From Digital Anxiety to Empowerment in Older Adults. **Finding**: Family support (step-by-step guidance) is the #1 buffer against digital anxiety. Moderate empowering support improves outcomes; excessive dependence may fuel learned helplessness.

- **Saudi Arabia (2025)**. Attitudes of Older Adult Population Toward Mobile Health Applications. 500 participants. **Finding**: Digital literacy, age-related limitations, and fear of mistakes hinder adoption. Family support, trust, and perceived app compatibility with daily life facilitate use.

- **Malaysia (2025)**. Intergenerational instructional strategies and older adult preferences. 26 older adults + 13 young instructors. **Finding**: Despite government digital initiatives, gaps persist. Intergenerational programs are most effective for learning.

## Device Preferences

- **Frontiers in Psychology (2017)**. Older Adults' Perceptions and Barriers to Tablet Computers. Focus groups. **Finding**: No consensus on tablet size — some prefer portability (small), others screen size (large).

- **Innovation in Aging / Oxford (2022)**. Systematic Review of Smartphone and Tablet Use by Older Adults. **Finding**: Stroke group with motor impairments preferred iPads/tablets due to large screen and ease of use.

- **Frontiers in Psychology (2018)**. Older adults may prefer tablets over traditional computers for portability and usability (adjustable font/icon size).

- **Fox News / Gerontologist (2014)**. Using iPads Could Help Older Adults' Thinking Abilities. 54 participants, ages 60-90. iPad selected because touch screen is easy to read, user-friendly menu, no mouse required. Cognitive benefits maintained at 1-year follow-up.

## China's Age-Friendly Design Regulations

- **China MIIT (2021)**. Guidelines for age-friendliness modifications: larger/adjustable text, text-to-speech, audio verification, ban on plug-in ad links in senior mode.

- **WeChat Care Mode (2021)**. Enlarged text, changed color scheme for recognition, larger buttons for main functions. 4+ million users activated.

- **PRNewswire (2021)**. Senior mode: no mobile ads, no payment-luring designs. Users 65+ reach human agent without complex IVR. Dialect recognition systems improved for non-Mandarin speakers.

## European Accessibility Act

- **Directive (EU) 2019/882**. Enforceable since June 28, 2025. Covers ATMs, smartphones, e-books, e-commerce, banking, transportation. Both public and private sector. Standard: EN 301 549 (based on WCAG 2.1 AA). Non-compliance: fines and penalties.

---

## Korea & Japan

- **South Korea (2025)**. JMIR Research Protocols. One of world's fastest aging societies; projected 50% of households led by older adults by 2047. Key barriers: low digital literacy, interface complexity, AI mistrust.

- **Korea (2025)**. PMC. "Super-aged" society by 2025 (>20% aged 65+). Global digital innovation leader yet high digital exclusion risk among older adults.

- **Japan Society 5.0**. Cabinet Office (2020). "Super-smart society" vision integrating technology with human empathy for all generations.

## Key Systematic Reviews (Global)

1. **Amouzadeh et al. (2025)**. Optimizing mobile app design for older adults. *Aging Clinical and Experimental Research* / Springer. Iran (Tabriz/Hamadan). 1,556 records → 132 articles. Most comprehensive review to date.

2. **Gomez-Hernandez et al. (2023)**. Design Guidelines of Mobile Apps for Older Adults. *JMIR mHealth and uHealth*. Spain / Australia / China. Most cited guidelines paper.

3. **NNGroup (2001, 2013, 2024)**. Three rounds of research, 123 participants aged 65+, five countries (US, Canada, Australia, Germany, Japan) over ~20 years. 87 design guidelines.

4. **Wickramathilaka et al. (2025)**. Accessibility Recommendations for Designing Better Mobile Application UIs for Seniors. Monash University, Australia. Two focus group studies (16 + 22 seniors). Personas + AdaptForge adaptive UI toolset.
